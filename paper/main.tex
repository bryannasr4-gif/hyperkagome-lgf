\title{Lattice Green's function of the hyperkagome lattice:\\
an irreducible third-order Picard--Fuchs operator and the absence of an elliptic closed form}

\author{Bryan Nasr}
\affiliation{Independent researcher\\ \texttt{bryannasr4@gmail.com}}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
The lattice Green's functions (LGFs) of the simple-cubic, body- and face-centred cubic lattices are classical:
each is the symmetric square of a second-order (elliptic) operator and admits a closed form in complete elliptic
integrals. The three-dimensional \emph{hyperkagome} lattice---the network of corner-sharing triangles realised
by the Ir sublattice of the spin-liquid candidate Na$_4$Ir$_3$O$_8$---has resisted such a treatment: Varma and
Monien [Phys.\ Rev.\ E \textbf{87}, 032109 (2013)] reduced its density of states to a threefold integral they
``found no way to exactly solve.'' We determine its analytic status. Working from exact lattice moments, we show that the
hyperkagome LGF $G(z)$ (after removing the trivial flat-band pole and exploiting an exact spectral symmetry) is
annihilated by a \emph{third-order} Picard--Fuchs operator $M$ of polynomial degree fifteen, which we determine by
differential approximants and certify to the guess-and-verify standard of this field---by massive overdetermination
(exact annihilation over $\mathbb{Q}$ through order $t^{111}$: $112$ relations against the $57$ free coefficients of
$M$, an overdetermination margin of $55$) and an independent from-scratch reconstruction (an unconditional
creative-telescoping proof that $M$ is the minimal annihilator remains open). The operator $M$ shows no first- or
second-order right factor over $\mathbb{Q}(t)$, and---by a Galois-descent argument---is irreducible over
$\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$; and it is \emph{not} a symmetric square of a second-order operator---its Frobenius
exponents $\{-\tfrac12,0,1\}$ at the band edge and $\{-1,-\tfrac12,\tfrac12\}$ at the principal van Hove point
$t=1$ ($E=0,2$) are not the arithmetic progressions a symmetric-square structure would force. By the
differential-Galois classification of third-order operators, an irreducible operator that is not a symmetric square
has no basis of complete elliptic integrals---this is what excludes an elliptic closed form; separately, a genuine
logarithmic solution (from the repeated exponent $-1$ at $t=0$) excludes finite and imprimitive Galois groups, so
$M$ is also non-Liouvillian, which excludes any algebraic or elementary closed form. Consequently, in sharp
contrast to the cubic lattices, the hyperkagome LGF has \emph{no closed form in algebraic, elementary, or elliptic
functions}: it is a genuine order-three Fuchsian period (Calabi--Yau type in the broad sense), not an elliptic one.
We give the operator, its complete Riemann scheme, the band-edge van Hove exponents, and the exact value
$\mathrm{Re}\,G(1)=1/9$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The lattice Green's function (LGF)
\begin{equation}
G(z)=\int_{\mathrm{BZ}}\frac{\rmd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,\frac{1}{z-\varepsilon(\bm k)}
=\int\frac{\rho(E)\,\rmd E}{z-E},
\label{eq:lgf}
\end{equation}
the resolvent of a tight-binding band $\varepsilon(\bm k)$ averaged over the Brillouin zone, is a central object
of lattice statistics: it controls random-walk return probabilities, defect and impurity states, and the local
density of states $\rho(E)=-\pi^{-1}\,\mathrm{Im}\,G(E+i0^+)$~\cite{Economou,Guttmann2010}. For the
three-dimensional cubic lattices the LGF is a celebrated closed form. Watson's triple
integral for the simple-cubic (SC) lattice~\cite{Watson1939}, and the body- and face-centred analogues, are all
expressible through products of complete elliptic integrals $K$~\cite{Joyce1973,GlasserZucker1977,Joyce1998}; structurally,
each of these order-three operators is the \emph{symmetric square} of a second-order (elliptic) operator, which
is precisely why an elliptic closed form exists.

Not every lattice is so fortunate. As the local geometry becomes more intricate---decorated, frustrated, or
multi-band lattices---the LGF can fail to reduce to elliptic integrals and instead becomes a period of a
higher-order or irreducible differential operator (the diamond lattice already signals this
trend~\cite{Guttmann2010,Joyce1998}). The natural three-dimensional frustrated network is the
\emph{hyperkagome} lattice: a chiral arrangement of corner-sharing triangles, obtained by removing one quarter
of the sites of the pyrochlore lattice in an ordered (space group $P4_132$) fashion. It is realised by the Ir$^{4+}$
moments of Na$_4$Ir$_3$O$_8$, a leading three-dimensional quantum-spin-liquid candidate~\cite{Okamoto2007,
Hopkinson2007,Lawler2008}, which has made its single-particle spectral functions of independent interest.

Varma and Monien~\cite{VarmaMonien2013} computed the hyperkagome density of states and reduced it to a single
threefold integral of a rational function of $\cos k_x,\cos k_y,\cos k_z$, remarking that ``it seems highly
suggestive that a closed form expression can be obtained if a particular complicated yet highly symmetrical
integral can be solved,'' but that ``we have currently found no way to exactly solve'' it. That integral has
remained open.

We settle the question. The hyperkagome LGF is governed by an \emph{irreducible third-order} Picard--Fuchs
operator that is \emph{not} a symmetric square; therefore, unlike the cubic lattices, it has \emph{no} closed
form in complete elliptic integrals. We obtain the operator explicitly, certify it, and read off its analytic
structure---thereby explaining \emph{why} an elliptic closed form was never found, and replacing it with the
exact object that does control the LGF.

\section{Hyperkagome lattice and its Green's function}
The hyperkagome lattice has twelve sites per cubic cell, each with coordination number four; its nearest-neighbour
tight-binding Bloch Hamiltonian $H(\bm k)$ is a $12\times12$ Hermitian matrix. We fix the hopping $t=1$ and the
energy origin at the band centre. Diagonalising $H(\bm k)$ over the Brillouin zone yields the spectrum shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:dos}. Three exact features organise everything that follows.

\emph{(i) A flat band, and the line-graph structure.} The hyperkagome lattice is the line graph of a three-regular
``premedial'' net $\Gamma$ (eight vertices per cell, twelve edges): its sites are the edges of $\Gamma$ and its
triangles are the vertices of $\Gamma$. For the line graph $L(\Gamma)$ of a $d$-regular graph one has
$A(L(\Gamma))=B^{\!\top}B-2I$ with $B$ the incidence matrix, so the spectrum is $\{\mu_i+d-2\}$ together with the
eigenvalue $-2$ of multiplicity $(\text{edges})-(\text{vertices})$. Here $d=3$, giving four flat bands at $E=-2$
(spectral weight exactly $1/3$, i.e.\ $\tfrac{12-8}{12}$); this contributes a pole $\tfrac13(z+2)^{-1}$ to $G(z)$
and is the flat-band pole written $1/(t_{\rm VM}{+}1)$ in Ref.~\cite{VarmaMonien2013}, whose energy variable is
$t_{\rm VM}=E/2$ (the correspondence, including this pole and its weight, is reproduced in their variable in the
Supplemental Material). The eight dispersive bands are $E=\mu_i+1$ and fill $[-2,4]$, since the adjacency
eigenvalues $\mu_i$ of a three-regular graph lie in $[-3,3]$.

\emph{(ii) An exact reflection symmetry, from bipartiteness.} A graph has an adjacency spectrum symmetric about
$0$ if and only if it is bipartite; we verify that the premedial net $\Gamma$ is bipartite (it admits a
translation-consistent two-colouring, checked on finite blocks up to $5^3$ cells, and its cycles are of even
length). Through the line-graph shift $E=\mu+1$ this makes the dispersive density of states \emph{exactly}
symmetric about $E=1$; independently, all odd central moments
$\int\rho_{\rm disp}(E)(E-1)^{2m+1}\rmd E$ vanish in exact arithmetic to order $239$. Hence the dispersive part of
$G$ is odd in $\zeta\equiv z-1$, and is a function of $t\equiv\zeta^{-2}$.

\emph{(iii) Integer moments.} The per-site spectral moments $m_n=\frac{1}{12}\Tr H^n$ are exact integers,
$m_{0..10}=1,0,4,4,28,60,260,756,2828,9292,33384$; we computed them to $m_{230}$ by closed-walk enumeration.
Among the ordered quarter-depletions of the pyrochlore lattice, the chiral hyperkagome enantiomorphs are the ones
reproducing this sequence (hexagon-containing depletions already differ at $m_6=264\neq260$); this selection is
checked separately and is not needed for what follows.

\section{The Picard--Fuchs operator}
Because $G(z)$ is the diagonal of a rational function over the torus, it is holonomic (D-finite): it satisfies a
linear differential equation with polynomial coefficients~\cite{Lipshitz1988}. We determine that equation from
the moments. Removing the flat band, $d_n=m_n-\tfrac13(-2)^n$, and forming the even central moments
$\nu_m=\int\rho_{\rm disp}(E-1)^{2m}\rmd E$, the generating function $\Phi(t)=\sum_m\nu_m t^m$ (with
$t=\zeta^{-2}$) is the essential object. Guessing a minimal annihilator from the exact $\nu$ sequence by the
method of differential approximants~\cite{Guttmann2010,orealgebra} returns an operator $L$ of order four and
degree fifteen.

This $L$ is reducible in a trivial way: it admits the constant solution, so $L=M\,\rmd/\rmd t$ exactly, with
$M$ of order \emph{three} and degree fifteen,
\begin{equation}
M=\sum_{i=0}^{3}c_i(t)\,\Big(\frac{\rmd}{\rmd t}\Big)^{i},\quad \deg c_i\le 15,
\label{eq:M}
\end{equation}
the minimal operator annihilating $\Phi'$. Its leading coefficient factorises as
\begin{equation}
c_3(t)\propto t^2\,(t-\tfrac19)(t-\tfrac15)(t-\tfrac14)(t-1)^3\,p_7(t),
\label{eq:lc}
\end{equation}
with $p_7$ an irreducible degree-seven polynomial. The finite singular points are images of the band structure
under $t=(E-1)^{-2}$: the band edge $E=4$ (equivalently $E=-2$) maps to $t=\tfrac19$, the principal van Hove
energies $E=0,2$ to $t=1$, and the interior van Hove features $E=-1,3$ and $E=1\pm\sqrt5$ to $t=\tfrac14$ and
$t=\tfrac15$; the half-integer exponent in \eqref{eq:riemann} confirms a square-root singularity at each. The
remaining singular points ($t=0$, $t=\infty$, and the degree-seven locus $p_7$) all appear in the complete Riemann
scheme \eqref{eq:riemann} below.

\emph{Certification.} The operator $M$ was reconstructed exactly over $\mathbb{Q}$ and shown to annihilate the
series $\Phi'(t)$ \emph{exactly over $\mathbb{Q}$} through order $t^{111}$---$112$ independent relations against
the $57$ free coefficients of $M$, an overdetermination margin of $55$ with zero residuals---a verification we
confirmed modulo three independent large primes. It was
furthermore re-derived from an entirely independent rebuild of the lattice (an independent quarter-depletion
construction, independent closed-walk moment enumeration to $m_{240}$, and an independent guess): the moment
sequence and all four coefficient polynomials of $M$ are bit-identical. As a further independent check, the
integer moments were reproduced by direct diagonalisation of the $12\times12$ Bloch Hamiltonian on a
Brillouin-zone grid---a method disjoint from the closed-walk enumeration. This is the standard
``conjectured-then-verified'' certification of the lattice-statistics literature~\cite{Guttmann2010}. An
unconditional derivation by creative telescoping~\cite{Koutschan2013,Bostan2013} from the rational integrand of
the resolvent---which would upgrade this verification to a formal proof---is a natural next step, well suited to
a dedicated holonomic-functions implementation.

\section{Riemann scheme and the absence of a closed form}
The analytic nature of the LGF is decided by the local structure of $M$. Computing the Frobenius indicial
exponents at each regular singular point gives the Riemann scheme
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
\begin{array}{c|ccccccc}
 & t=0 & t=\tfrac19 & t=\tfrac15 & t=\tfrac14 & t=1 & p_7 & t=\infty \\\hline
\text{exp.} & \{-1,-1,0\} & \{-\tfrac12,0,1\} & \{-\tfrac12,0,1\} & \{-\tfrac12,0,1\}
 & \{-1,-\tfrac12,\tfrac12\} & \{0,1,3\} & \{\tfrac32,2,3\}
\end{array}
\label{eq:riemann}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
Here $p_7$ denotes the irreducible degree-seven locus, whose seven Galois-conjugate roots all carry the same
integer exponents $\{0,1,3\}$, computed exactly by reduction modulo $p_7$. These are \emph{apparent}
singularities: integer exponents alone do not establish this, but all three local solutions there are log-free,
which we verify from the Frobenius obstruction at each root of $p_7$ over several primes. We use the standard
Riemann-scheme convention at infinity, in which an exponent $\rho$ at $t=\infty$ corresponds to a local solution
$y\sim t^{-\rho}$; the three solutions there thus behave as $t^{-3/2}$, $t^{-2}$ and $t^{-3}$. As a check on
\eqref{eq:riemann}, the operator has $s=13$ singular points---the five rational points above, the seven roots of
$p_7$, and $t=\infty$; the relation counts the apparent singularities on $p_7$ along with the rest---and the
exponents sum to
\begin{equation}
-2 + 3\cdot\tfrac12 - 1 + 7\cdot 4 + \tfrac{13}{2} \;=\; 33 \;=\; \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}\,(s-2),\qquad n=3,
\end{equation}
so the Fuchs relation is satisfied exactly.

The repeated exponent $-1$ at $t=0$ is the source of the logarithmic solution used
below. The half-integer exponent $-\tfrac12$ at the band edge $t=\tfrac19$ is the square-root van Hove singularity
of a three-dimensional band edge, $\rho(E)\sim\sqrt{E_{\rm edge}-E}$.

Three facts now determine the answer.

\emph{$M$ is irreducible over $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$.} $M$ is Fuchsian---regular singular at every point,
including $t=\infty$ (exponents $\{\tfrac32,2,3\}$)---so any first-order right factor $\mathrm{d}/\mathrm{d}t-u$
has $u=\sum_s e_s/(t-s)$ with $e_s$ a local exponent at each singular point $s$ and \emph{no} polynomial part. At
the rational singular points the $e_s$ are the exponents of \eqref{eq:riemann}; at the irreducible degree-seven
locus $p_7$ the common (Galois-invariant) residue ranges over its exponents $\{0,1,3\}$. This candidate set is
finite, and each candidate is tested exactly over $\mathbb{Q}$ by the
divisibility criterion $c_3(u''+3uu'+u^3)+c_2(u'+u^2)+c_1u+c_0=0$. No first-order right factor of $M$ exists over
$\mathbb{Q}(t)$, and none of $\mathrm{adjoint}(M)$---which for a third-order operator excludes an order-two right
factor of $M$ as well---so $M$ has no right factor of order one or two over $\mathbb{Q}(t)$. We now descend to
$\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$ in two steps. \emph{First}, $M$ has no order-one right factor there: a hyperexponential
solution $\mathrm{d}/\mathrm{d}t-u$ with $u\in\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$ has a finite Galois orbit
$\{\mathrm{d}/\mathrm{d}t-u^\sigma\}$ of right factors of $M$, whose least common left multiple is a right factor
\emph{defined over $\mathbb{Q}(t)$} of order $k\in\{1,2,3\}$; $k=1$ gives $u\in\mathbb{Q}(t)$ and $k=2$ an
order-two right factor over $\mathbb{Q}$, both excluded above, while $k=3$ would make $M$ the LCLM of order-one
operators, hence completely reducible, forcing \emph{semisimple} local monodromy and no logarithm---contradicting
the genuine logarithm at $t=0$ (repeated indicial exponent $-1$, exactly one log-free local solution of three,
verified directly). \emph{Second}, $M$ has no order-two right factor over $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$: such a factor
is equivalent to an order-one right factor of $\mathrm{adjoint}(M)$ over $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$, excluded by the
identical descent applied to $\mathrm{adjoint}(M)$ (no order-one factor over $\mathbb{Q}$ by the adjoint search, no
order-two by the $M$ search, and $\mathrm{adjoint}(M)$ is not completely reducible, since complete reducibility is
self-dual and $M$ is not). Hence $M$ is irreducible over $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}(t)$.
(This exact enumeration replaces the floating-point candidate search of earlier drafts; the computation is provided
in the Supplemental Material and is independently reproducible with a certified factorizer such as Maple's
\texttt{DFactor} or Magma.)

\emph{$M$ is not a symmetric square.} If $M$ were the symmetric square of a second-order operator with local
exponents $\{a,b\}$, its three exponents would be $\{2a,a+b,2b\}$, an arithmetic progression whose middle term is
the mean of the outer two. No triple in \eqref{eq:riemann} is of this form: at $t=\tfrac19$ the exponents
$\{-\tfrac12,0,1\}$ have mean of outer terms $\tfrac14\neq0$, and at $t=1$ the exponents $\{-1,-\tfrac12,\tfrac12\}$
have mean of outer terms $-\tfrac14\neq-\tfrac12$; a single such point already suffices. Because a gauge
transformation shifts the three exponents at each point by a constant depending only on that point, and an
algebraic pullback multiplies them by the local ramification index---both operations preserving arithmetic
progressions at each point, which a symmetric square must have at
\emph{every} point---this excludes not only a symmetric square but every gauge- or pullback-equivalent of one. As a
positive control, the same analysis applied to the simple-cubic LGF returns the symmetric-square-compatible
exponents $\{0,\tfrac12,1\}=\{2a,a+b,2b\}$ with $\{a,b\}=\{0,\tfrac12\}$, correctly reflecting its closed form in
$K$.

\emph{No elliptic closed form.} By the differential-Galois classification of third-order linear operators, an
irreducible operator whose solutions are expressible through complete elliptic integrals (equivalently, through
periods of a single second-order/elliptic operator) must be the symmetric square of that second-order
operator~\cite{SingerUlmer,vdPutSinger}; irreducibility already rules out a single $K$ and products of $K$ at
different moduli (whose operator would be reducible or of order four). Since $M$ is irreducible and not a
symmetric square, it has no such basis. This mirrors the contrast with the cubic lattices, whose order-three
LGF operators are precisely symmetric squares of elliptic operators~\cite{BoukraaHassaniMaillard2014}.

\emph{No algebraic or elementary closed form.} On a separate axis from the elliptic question, $M$ has no
\emph{Liouvillian} solutions, hence no closed form in algebraic or elementary functions. By the Singer--Ulmer
classification, an irreducible third-order operator has Liouvillian solutions only if its differential Galois group
is finite or imprimitive. A finite group makes every solution algebraic, hence log-free. An imprimitive group
permutes three lines, so in the corresponding basis every element---in particular every local monodromy, since for
a Fuchsian operator the Galois group is the Zariski closure of the monodromy group---is a monomial matrix. Such a
matrix is necessarily semisimple: if its permutation part has order $k$, its $k$-th power is diagonal and
invertible, and an invertible matrix with a diagonalisable power is itself diagonalisable in characteristic zero.
In either case there can be \emph{no logarithmic solution}. But $M$ has a genuine logarithmic solution:
at $t=0$ the indicial exponent $-1$ is repeated and only one log-free (Laurent) local solution exists (of three),
so the local monodromy is non-semisimple. This excludes both the finite and the imprimitive cases, so the Galois
group acts irreducibly and non-solvably and $M$ is non-Liouvillian. Being non-Liouvillian does \emph{not} by itself
preclude an elliptic form---the complete elliptic integral $K$ is itself non-Liouvillian, and the simple-cubic LGF
operator is irreducible and non-Liouvillian yet closed-form in $K$; the elliptic exclusion for the hyperkagome
comes solely from the not-symmetric-square property established above. The two independent legs together give the
result.

\emph{Conclusion.} The hyperkagome LGF is a period of an irreducible third-order Picard--Fuchs operator that is
not a symmetric square and is non-Liouvillian; it therefore has \emph{no closed form in algebraic, elementary, or
elliptic functions}, in contrast to the SC, BCC and FCC lattices. It is instead a genuine order-three Fuchsian
period with six non-apparent singular points ($t=0,\tfrac19,\tfrac15,\tfrac14,1,\infty$), together with the seven
apparent singularities on $p_7$, of Calabi--Yau type in the broad
sense~\cite{AESZ}. (It is not a $\,_3F_2$ operator, which would have only three singular points; we do not claim it
is inexpressible in every higher special-function class.) This is the exact sense in which the Varma--Monien
integral cannot be ``solved'': the obstruction is structural. The statement is conditional on $M$ being the minimal
annihilator of the LGF---established here to the guess-and-verify standard of the field, with an unconditional
creative-telescoping proof the natural next step---so we phrase it as settling the analytic status of the
hyperkagome LGF rather than as exhibiting a closed form.

\section{Exact results}
Although no elliptic closed form exists, the operator yields exact data. At the symmetric centre $E=1$ the LGF
takes the exact value
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Re}\,G(1)=\tfrac19,
\end{equation}
the flat-band contribution $\tfrac13/(1+2)=\tfrac19$ plus a dispersive principal value that vanishes by the
$E=1$ symmetry. (The value at $E=2$ is a singular boundary value: $E=2$ is the van Hove point $t=1$ of
\eqref{eq:riemann}, where $\mathrm{Im}\,G$ diverges logarithmically.) From the exponent $-\tfrac12$ at
$t=\tfrac19$ the band-edge density of states behaves as $\rho(E)\sim\sqrt{E_{\rm edge}-E}$, and the flat band
contributes the delta function $\tfrac13\,\delta(E+2)$.

\section{Discussion}
We have given the exact differential equation that controls the hyperkagome lattice Green's function and shown,
constructively, that it is governed by an irreducible third-order Picard--Fuchs operator with no symmetric-square
factorisation. This settles the analytic status of the integral left open by Varma and Monien: a closed form in
complete elliptic integrals does not exist, because the operator that the cubic lattices share---a symmetric
square of an elliptic operator---is replaced for the hyperkagome by an irreducible operator whose Riemann scheme
\eqref{eq:riemann} structurally forbids it. The hyperkagome thus joins the family of frustrated and decorated
lattices whose Green's functions are genuine higher-period objects rather than elliptic ones.

The result is directly relevant to the modelling of Na$_4$Ir$_3$O$_8$ and related hyperkagome magnets, where the
single-particle LGF enters impurity, disorder, and dynamical-mean-field treatments; it provides the exact
band-edge and special-point data and a certified equation for high-precision evaluation. More broadly, it adds an
explicit three-dimensional frustrated lattice to the catalogue of Picard--Fuchs operators arising in lattice
statistics. An unconditional creative-telescoping proof, the explicit identification of $M$ within the
Calabi--Yau operator database, and the analogous Heisenberg/transport Green's functions are natural next steps.

All lattice constructions, exact moments, the certified operator, and the verification scripts are provided in
the Supplemental Material and a public repository.
